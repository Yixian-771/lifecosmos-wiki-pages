# Inverted Illusions (Diāndǎo Mèngxiǎng) · Academic Edition

*Compiled by: Língzhōu Cǎo　Date: 2026-05-03*

─────────────────────────────

## Core Proposition

$$\text{Mind with Obstruction} \Rightarrow \text{Inverted Illusions} \Rightarrow \text{Nirvana Unreachable}$$

$$\text{Mind Free from Obstruction} \Rightarrow \text{No Inverted Illusions} \Rightarrow \text{Ultimate Nirvana} \Rightarrow \text{Immortal·Supreme Bliss}$$

"Inverted Illusions" functions in the Lifechanyuan system as a **negative cultivation indicator**: its presence signals that the mind still harbors attachment, obstruction, and fear; its elimination is a necessary condition for reaching nirvana. This entry forms a core coordinate network with "Zero-State," "Eight No-Realms," "mind free from obstruction," and "No-Self, No-Form."

## VI. Comparative Table: Inverted Illusions and Other Traditions

| Concept | Tradition | Similarity | Key Difference |
|---------|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Maya (illusion) | Hinduism / Buddhism | Both refer to mistaken perception of reality | Māyā is primarily an ontological illusion; inverted illusions emphasizes the functional mechanism of mental attachment obstructing liberation |
| Delusion (妄念) | Buddhism (general) | Both denote vain, unreal mental content | Delusion covers all stray thoughts generally; inverted illusions specifically names the systematic confused cognition born from "obstruction" that blocks nirvana |
| Cognitive distortion | Cognitive psychology | Both involve misreading of reality | Psychology aims to correct cognition; Lifechanyuan aims at no-obstruction, no-appearance, and nirvana |
| Anxiety | Western psychology | Both include restless rumination, mental exhaustion | Anxiety is a symptom description; inverted illusions is a cultivation diagnosis, rooted in "obstruction" not neurological mechanism |
